# Pitch Deck Best Practices - VC Perspectives

## Essential Pitch Deck Structure

A winning pitch deck typically has 10-15 slides covering these key areas:

### Slide 1: Introduction
- Company name and tagline
- Your one-sentence elevator pitch
- Founder names and roles

### Slide 2: Problem
- Clearly articulate the pain point
- Quantify the problem (e.g., "$50B wasted annually on...")
- Show you deeply understand customer pain
- Use storytelling or a customer anecdote

### Slide 3: Solution
- Introduce your product
- Show how it solves the problem
- Include visuals (screenshots, demo, diagram)
- Explain the "aha moment"

### Slide 4: Market Opportunity
- Total Addressable Market (TAM)
- Serviceable Available Market (SAM)
- Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)
- Use bottom-up analysis (# customers Ã— price)
- Show market growth trends

### Slide 5: Product/Technology
- Key features and differentiation
- Technology innovation
- Product roadmap
- Any IP or patents

### Slide 6: Traction
- Revenue metrics
- User growth
- Key partnerships
- Customer testimonials
- Show momentum (growth curves)

### Slide 7: Business Model
- How you make money
- Pricing strategy
- Unit economics (CAC, LTV)
- Revenue streams
- Path to profitability

### Slide 8: Competition
- Competitive landscape
- Your positioning (use a 2x2 matrix)
- Why you'll win
- Barriers to entry

### Slide 9: Go-to-Market Strategy
- Customer acquisition channels
- Sales strategy
- Marketing approach
- Partnership strategy

### Slide 10: Team
- Founders' backgrounds
- Key hires
- Advisors/Board
- Why you're uniquely qualified

### Slide 11: Financials
- Historical revenue (if any)
- 3-5 year projections
- Key assumptions
- Burn rate and runway

### Slide 12: The Ask
- How much you're raising
- Use of funds breakdown
- Key milestones you'll achieve
- Expected runway

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Too Much Text
- VCs scan, they don't read
- Use bullet points, not paragraphs
- Let visuals tell the story
- One key idea per slide

### 2. Unclear Problem
- Don't assume VCs understand your market
- Avoid jargon
- Make the pain tangible
- Use concrete examples

### 3. Made-Up Market Sizes
- Don't cite $1T markets
- Use credible sources
- Show bottom-up calculations
- Be realistic about your obtainable market

### 4. Ignoring Competition
- Saying "we have no competitors" is a red flag
- Show you understand the landscape
- Explain why incumbents won't crush you
- Highlight your unique advantages

### 5. Weak Team Slide
- Don't list generic skills
- Highlight relevant experience
- Show founder-market fit
- Explain why you'll win

### 6. Unrealistic Financials
- "We'll capture 1% of a $100B market" is lazy
- Build bottom-up projections
- Explain your assumptions
- Show you understand unit economics

### 7. No Clear Ask
- Be specific about funding needs
- Show how you'll use the money
- Explain the milestones you'll hit
- Make it easy for VCs to say yes

## What VCs Actually Care About

### Early Stage (Seed)
1. Team quality and domain expertise
2. Problem significance
3. Market size
4. Early traction or validation
5. Clarity of vision

### Growth Stage (Series A+)
1. Product-market fit proof
2. Unit economics
3. Growth rate
4. Competitive position
5. Capital efficiency

## Storytelling Tips

### Use the "Before and After" Framework
- Show the world before your solution
- Demonstrate the transformation
- Paint a vision of the future

### Make It Personal
- Share your "why" - what drives you
- Use customer stories
- Show passion and authenticity

### Create Urgency
- Why now? Why is this the right time?
- Market timing is critical
- Show the wave you're riding

## Presentation Delivery

### Dos:
- Start with a hook (surprising stat, story, demo)
- Maintain eye contact
- Show passion and conviction
- Invite questions throughout
- Have backup slides ready
- Know your numbers cold

### Don'ts:
- Don't read slides
- Don't apologize for rough edges
- Don't bash competitors
- Don't avoid tough questions
- Don't go over time

## Questions VCs Will Ask

Be prepared for:

**About the Business:**
- Who's your target customer?
- How do you acquire customers?
- What's your CAC and LTV?
- How do you compare to [competitor]?
- What keeps you up at night?

**About the Market:**
- How big is this market really?
- Is it growing or shrinking?
- What are the trends?
- Who are the incumbents?

**About the Team:**
- Why are you the right founder?
- Who are you looking to hire?
- What gaps exist in your team?
- Have you worked together before?

**About the Raise:**
- How much runway will this give you?
- What metrics will you hit?
- When will you raise again?
- What's the valuation?

## Design and Visuals

- Use consistent branding
- High-quality images and graphics
- Clean, modern layout
- Minimal color palette (2-3 colors)
- Large, readable fonts (24pt+)
- Charts over tables
- White space is your friend

## Appendix Slides

Include backup slides for:
- Detailed financials
- Technology architecture
- Customer case studies
- Press mentions
- Detailed competitive analysis
- Team bios
- Risk factors and mitigation

## Final Checklist

Before you pitch:
- [ ] Problem is crystal clear
- [ ] Solution is compelling
- [ ] Market size is believable
- [ ] Traction is demonstrated
- [ ] Business model makes sense
- [ ] Competition is addressed
- [ ] Team is impressive
- [ ] Ask is specific
- [ ] Deck is under 15 slides
- [ ] No typos or errors
- [ ] Financials are defensible
- [ ] Story flows logically

Remember: Your deck gets you the meeting. Your story and metrics get you the investment.
